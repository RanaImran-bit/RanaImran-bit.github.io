About

I am Mushtaq Rana Imran, a PhD research student at Harbin Institute of Technology, Shenzhen. My research primarily focuses on exploring altermagnetism in kagome lattices and investigating their superconducting and magnetic properties. I also engage in machine learning and neural networks to complement my theoretical work.

I hold a Master of Science in Physics from Ludong University, where I conducted research in condensed matter physics, Monte Carlo simulations, and stochastic processes. Additionally, I earned my Bachelor's degree in Physics from the University of the Punjab, Lahore, with a focus on numerical modeling techniques.

My academic interests lie at the intersection of theoretical and computational physics, and I am actively seeking research collaboration opportunities to contribute to advancements in condensed matter physics through innovative and cutting-edge research.

Background

I have always been deeply fascinated by the intersection of theory and computation in physics. My academic journey started with a Bachelor's degree in Physics from the University of the Punjab, Lahore, where I developed a strong foundation in theoretical and experimental physics. My undergraduate research focused on numerical modeling techniques, particularly using computational methods to analyze physical systems.

Building on this foundation, I pursued a Master of Science in Physics at Ludong University. During my Master's, I worked on condensed matter physics, Monte Carlo simulations, and stochastic processes. This experience deepened my interest in computational methods and their application to complex systems, including strongly correlated electron systems. I became particularly interested in the role of computational models in understanding emergent phenomena like superconductivity and magnetism.

Currently, as a PhD student at Harbin Institute of Technology, Shenzhen, I am focusing on altermagnetism in kagome lattices and the role of next-nearest-neighbor hopping in shaping magnetism and pairing in these systems. I use advanced computational techniques, including Monte Carlo simulations and quantum many-body methods, to study these phenomena. My research also extends into machine learning and neural networks, where I apply these tools to develop more efficient methods for simulating quantum systems and solving complex physical problems.

Research Interests

My research interests lie in the following areas:

  1. Study of novel phenomena of magnetism and superconductivity in the quantum model.
  2. AI for science applications in quantum physics.
  3. Employing quantum Monte Carlo, DFT material simulation, neural networks, and theoretical methods like mean-field analysis.
  4. Research on strongly correlated systems, quantum Monte Carlo, physics-based machine learning, and large model fusion research.
  5. Phase transition and Fermi velocity renormalization in graphene with Coulomb interaction, and pairing and topological properties in spin-orbit coupled fermions.

Research Areas

Quantum Filtering and Control in Open Systems

Machine-learning and neural ODE based approaches for modeling and controlling dissipative qubits, enabling real-time quantum state estimation and feedback control.

Altermagnetism and Strongly Correlated Quantum Systems

Magnetism and pairing in next-nearest-neighbor altermagnets, focusing on the interplay between electronic structure, superconductivity, and quantum phase transitions.

Stochastic Processes and Quantum Dynamics

Analytical and numerical studies of generalized Langevin dynamics and stochastic processes, with applications to quantum Monte Carlo and multi-dimensional systems.
